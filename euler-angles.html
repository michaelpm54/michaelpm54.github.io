Euler angles

Published: Sun 07 February 2021
By Michael McAssey

In Notes.

  • Pitch - Y axis

the goal is to have a vector that represents a front/facing vector of unit length: v^=(x,y,z)\hat{v} = (x, y, z)v^=(x,y,z)

We can feed this vector to pointing functions such as glm::lookAt or Quaternion.LookAt in Unity.

x=∣v∣cos⁡(yaw) x = |v|\cos(yaw) x=∣v∣cos(yaw)

In this case, ∣v∣|v|∣v∣ is 111 so it becomes

x=cos⁡(yaw) x = \cos(yaw) x=cos(yaw)

Similarly looking from above, Z can be thought of as

z=sin⁡(roll) z = \sin(roll) z=sin(roll)

And finally

y=sin⁡(pitch) y = \sin(pitch) y=sin(pitch)

But we need to simulate what actually happens, and these formulae alone aren't enough.

What isn't modelled is what happens to xxx as pitchpitchpitch changes. As your camera points up and up, the pitch increases but the xxx stays the same, which ultimately affects the end v^\hat{v}v^.

Imagine the pitch is completely vertical:

pitch=90°sin⁡(90°)=1 pitch = 90° \\ \sin(90°) = 1 pitch=90°sin(90°)=1

Say we have the facing vector v^=(0.5,1,0)\hat{v} = (0.5, 1, 0)v^=(0.5,1,0).
This isn't actually a valid unit vector because ∣(0.5,1,0)∣≈1.12|(0.5, 1, 0)| \approx 1.12∣(0.5,1,0)∣≈1.12. When modifying the yyy component, the other two values need to be scaled accordingly.

x=cos⁡(yaw)cos⁡(pitch)y=sin⁡(pitch)z=sin⁡(yaw)cos⁡(pitch) x = \cos(yaw) \cos(pitch) \\ y = \sin(pitch) \\ z = \sin(yaw) \cos(pitch) x=cos(yaw)cos(pitch)y=sin(pitch)z=sin(yaw)cos(pitch)

Which would produce ∣(0.5,1,0)∣≈1.12|(0.5, 1, 0)| \approx 1.12∣(0.5,1,0)∣≈1.12.
